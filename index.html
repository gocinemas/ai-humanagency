Concept 1

What is Artificial Intelligence?

AI is software that can learn from experience rather than following rigid, hand-written rules. Instead of a programmer writing "if it has pointy ears and whiskers, it's a cat," an AI system looks at millions of photos of cats until it figures out the pattern itself.

AI isn't magic or a conscious mind — it's pattern recognition at a massive scale. It's very good at certain tasks (spotting tumours in scans, translating languages) and completely hopeless at others (common sense, physical tasks).

Plain version: very smart pattern matching
📚
Concept 2

Machine Learning

Machine learning is how most AI is built. Instead of programming every rule, you feed the system thousands (or millions) of examples with correct answers — spam emails labelled "spam," tumour scans labelled "tumour" — and it works out the rules on its own.

The catch: it only ever learned from what it's seen. Ask it something truly new and it struggles. This is why AI can beat world champions at chess but can't load a dishwasher.

Plain version: learns by example, not by rules
🧠
Concept 3

Neural Networks

A neural network is the mathematical engine inside most modern AI. Loosely inspired by how neurons in the brain connect, it's made of layers of simple calculations that pass information forward, transforming raw input (pixels, words) into a useful output (a label, a sentence, a prediction).

"Deep learning" just means a neural network with many layers — the depth lets it learn much more complex patterns. Depth is why AI went from recognising digits to generating photorealistic faces.

Plain version: many layers of simple maths, stacked
💬
Concept 4

Large Language Models (LLMs)

The technology behind ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini. An LLM is trained on an enormous amount of text — books, websites, Wikipedia, code — and learns to predict what word comes next, billions of times over, until it can write, explain, translate, and reason with language fluently.

It doesn't understand in the way you do. It's a very sophisticated autocomplete — but that autocomplete turns out to be astonishingly useful.

Plain version: autocomplete trained on most of the internet
✨
Concept 5

Generative AI

Most older AI was discriminative — it classified things. Generative AI creates new things: text, images, music, video, code, voices. It's trained on vast amounts of human-made content and learns to produce new content in the same style.

This is why you can now type "a watercolour painting of a red panda drinking tea" and get a convincing image in seconds. The creativity isn't in the machine — the creativity is in what you ask it for.

Plain version: AI that makes things, not just recognises them
⚡
Concept 6

AI Agents

Until recently, AI mostly answered questions. Agents take action. An AI agent can be given a goal — "research competitors and write a report" — and will browse the web, read pages, take notes, and draft the report itself, without you doing each step manually.

This is the next big shift. Instead of AI being a very smart search box, it becomes something closer to a capable junior colleague that can actually get things done.

Plain version: AI that acts, not just answers
🗣️
Concept 7

Prompting

A prompt is what you type to an AI. It sounds simple but the way you phrase a request makes an enormous difference to the quality of the output. "Summarise this" gives a different result to "Summarise this in 3 bullet points, for someone who has never worked in marketing."

Good prompting isn't a secret skill — it's just being specific. Tell the AI who it's for, what format you want, what to avoid, and what you already know.

Plain version: how you talk to AI — specificity wins
⚠️
Concept 8

Hallucinations — When AI Gets It Wrong

AI systems sometimes confidently state things that are completely false — invented citations, made-up statistics, wrong dates. This is called hallucination. It happens because the model generates plausible-sounding text, not necessarily true text.

The fix: treat AI output the way you'd treat advice from a brilliant but overconfident friend. Useful for ideas and drafts — always verify facts that actually matter before acting on them.

Plain version: AI can be wrong and sound completely certain

Health & Medicine

AI can spot patterns in medical images that trained doctors miss — and do it in seconds across thousands of scans. It's also accelerating drug discovery from decades to years.

Cancer detection: AI reads mammograms and CT scans with accuracy matching radiologists
Drug discovery: Google's AlphaFold solved a 50-year protein-folding problem — compressing years of biology research
Admin: Automatically transcribes consultations so doctors spend time with patients, not paperwork
📖

Education

Every student learns differently. AI tutors can adapt in real-time — giving harder problems when you're ready, slowing down when you're not, and never making you feel embarrassed for asking the same question twice.

Personal tutoring: Khan Academy's Khanmigo gives one-to-one Socratic tutoring at no cost
Language learning: Duolingo uses AI to adapt difficulty and give instant pronunciation feedback
Essay feedback: Immediate, detailed feedback on drafts before a teacher even sees them
💳

Finance & Banking

AI watches every transaction in real-time for fraud patterns it learned from millions of past cases. It can also assess credit risk more fairly and give financial advice at scale.

Fraud detection: Visa blocks billions in fraud annually using AI pattern recognition
Credit decisions: AI assesses risk using broader data than traditional credit scores alone
Customer service: Chatbots resolve most banking queries instantly, 24/7
🎨

Creative Work

AI is a creative collaborator, not a replacement. Writers use it to beat blank-page paralysis. Designers generate a hundred variations in the time one used to take. Musicians sketch ideas they'd never have the technical skills to produce.

Copywriting: First drafts, taglines, and A/B test variations in minutes
Image generation: Concept art, product mock-ups, marketing visuals from a text description
Music: Generate background tracks, experiment with arrangements, or create fully original compositions
💻

Software Development

AI has changed how code is written more than anything since the internet. Developers now spend more time reviewing and guiding AI output than writing every line from scratch — and they ship faster for it.

Code completion: GitHub Copilot suggests entire functions as you type — used by millions of developers
Debugging: Paste an error and get an explanation and fix, instantly
Non-developers: People with no coding background now build working apps by describing what they want
⚖️

Legal & Compliance

Reading and summarising legal documents used to take expensive hours of lawyer time. AI can review a contract in seconds, flag unusual clauses, and explain what you're actually signing in plain English.

Contract review: Highlight non-standard terms and compare against templates automatically
Research: Find relevant case law across thousands of documents in seconds
Plain English: "What does this clause actually mean?" — now answerable instantly
🎧

Customer Service

Most customer service questions are the same questions, asked thousands of times a day. AI handles these instantly — leaving human agents free for the complex, emotional, or unusual cases where they actually add value.

Instant resolution: Order status, returns, FAQs — resolved without any wait
Tone matching: AI detects frustration and adjusts its responses accordingly
Multilingual: Serve customers in any language without needing specialist agents
🔬

Science & Research

Scientists are drowning in data. AI doesn't get tired, doesn't have biases about what's interesting, and can spot correlations across datasets too large for any human team to analyse.

Climate: Weather forecasting and climate modelling at resolutions previously impossible
Astronomy: Classifying millions of galaxies from telescope data automatically
Materials: Discovering new materials for batteries and solar cells by simulating millions of combinations

Alan Turing
Mathematician · 1912–1954

Before computers were common, Turing asked: can machines think? He proposed the Turing Test — if you can't tell whether you're chatting with a human or a machine, the machine has passed. He also invented much of the mathematical theory that modern computing runs on.

"We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done."
